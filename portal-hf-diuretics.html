❤️ Heart Failure Diuretic Management

Guidelines for the Administration of IV and SC Diuretics for Heart Failure Patients in the Community Setting

🏥 CUH Virtual Ward — Division C 📄 Version 2 • October 2025 👥 Butler, Ford & Bines
⚠️

Clinical Guideline — For Use by Trained Healthcare Professionals

This tool summarises the CUH Virtual Ward guideline for parenteral diuretics in heart failure. It does not replace clinical judgement. Always discuss with the Virtual Ward consultant or senior clinical team when uncertain.

📋 Scope ✅ Patient Selection 🔍 Assessment 💊 Choose Route 📊 Monitoring ⚠️ Escalation 🏠 Discharge

📋 Scope & Purpose

▼

These guidelines facilitate the administration of hospital-initiated diuretic therapy in patients' own homes under the management of the Virtual Ward at Cambridge University Hospitals NHS Foundation Trust.

This protocol offers three treatment options:

  1. Oral metolazone — first-line choice due to ease of administration and efficacy
  2. Intravenous (IV) furosemide — for patients requiring parenteral diuresis
  3. Subcutaneous (SC) furosemide — anticipated for patients in their last days to weeks of life
✅ Service aims

Reduce hospital admissions for decompensated heart failure, facilitate earlier discharge, reduce hospital-related complications (deconditioning, falls, delirium, hospital-acquired infection), and avoid ED attendances.

✅ Patient Selection

▼

Inclusion Criteria

0 of 8 confirmed

  • Accepted under the Virtual Ward for ongoing management
  • Heart failure confirmed as cause of symptoms (no reversible causes identified)
  • Fluid retention: peripheral oedema above the knee and/or weight gain >3kg above dry weight
  • Failure to relieve fluid overload by lifestyle measures including fluid restriction
  • Considered bumetanide switch and thiazide addition (bumetanide 1mg ≈ furosemide 40mg)
  • Sufficient and appropriate social/family/carer support
  • Patient able to give informed consent to diuretic therapy at home
  • Patient able to weigh themselves (or be weighed) and take daily observations (HR/BP/SpO2)

Exclusion Criteria

🛑 Do not proceed if any of the following apply:

  • Symptomatic hypotension — first consider reduction or withdrawal of antihypertensives (e.g. amlodipine). Avoid abrupt withdrawal of beta-blockers (risk of rebound tachycardia).
  • Insufficient carer support
  • Pulmonary oedema with uncontrolled respiratory distress and the patient is a candidate for escalation to intensive heart failure management under Cardiology. If expected as part of ongoing treatment, consider palliative symptom control (e.g. opioids).

🔍 Initial Assessment & Onboarding

▼

0 of 10 completed

  • On VW EPIC referral, request symptom monitoring: SOB, PND, orthopnoea, leg swelling
  • Document BP, HR, weight and target weight (EPIC synopsis useful)
  • Advise patient on fluid intake recording and any fluid restriction
  • Obtain baseline U&Es
  • Determine treatment route: metolazone, IV furosemide, or SC furosemide
  • Review infusion site if in-situ; check for signs of infection
  • Advise patient/carer on medication changes; no oral diuretics on day of IV administration
  • Consider toileting needs — commode or short-term catheter if mobility is an issue
  • Establish Treatment Escalation Plan (ReSPECT form) — ensure printed, signed copy with patient
  • Send discharge letter and onboarding documentation to GP immediately

Prescribe licensed metolazone (Xaqua®) — refer to prescribing support document for additional information.
2
Give stat dose: Metolazone 2.5mg
3
Daily monitoring as per monitoring guidance below.
4

✅ On completion

Optimise oral diuretics, review other HF medications (ACEi/ARB, SGLT2i, MRA) with GP advice on up-titration, send comprehensive discharge letter with current weight, target weight, and diuretic plan if weight increases.

💉 IV Furosemide Administration

⚠️ Key prescribing points

Maximum rate: 4mg/min. Commence no later than 4pm to avoid excess overnight toileting. Dilute in NaCl 0.9% if necessary. Glucose solutions are unsuitable. Oral diuretics have ~50–60% bioavailability vs 100% IV.

Dose Conversion: Oral → IV

Start at equivalent dose or increase by one increment if clinically appropriate:

Failure to respond

1
Increase loop diuretic by 40mg furosemide increments (max 240mg).
2
Consider adding metolazone — this will reduce the ongoing requirement for IV diuretic therapy.
3
If no response within 72 hours (or agreed timeframe) — discuss with patient regarding wishes and goals of care. Consider switching focus to comfort and dignity vs. further hospital-based management.
✅ On completion

Convert to appropriate oral dose. Review all HF medications with GP advice on continuation and up-titration (ACEi/ARB, SGLT2i, MRA). Send comprehensive discharge letter with current weight, target weight, U&E follow-up plan (usually within 1 week), and organised follow-up with local heart failure teams.

🏥 Subcutaneous Furosemide — End of Life

⚠️ Unlicensed use

SC furosemide is unlicensed in the UK but has been used with success (Dickman & Schneider 2016). For end-stage heart failure patients requiring parenteral diuresis for symptom control. Initiate alongside symptomatic management of dyspnoea (opiates and/or benzodiazepines) as necessary.

Inclusion criteria (SC route)

  • End-stage heart failure, unresponsive or unable to tolerate higher-dose oral or IV diuretics
  • Wish to avoid hospital admission; IV diuretics in the community cannot be facilitated
  • Last days of life requiring ongoing diuretics for symptom relief

Exclusion criteria (SC route)

  • No identifiable symptomatic benefit from high-dose IV or SC therapy
  • Severe site reaction to SC administration

Dosing

SC dosing is a 1:1 conversion from oral dose

e.g. 120mg furosemide orally daily = 120mg/24h via syringe pump. Note: oral bioavailability is ~60–70%, so this represents a dose increase. Use previous oral 24h requirement as starting dose and titrate to response. Convert bumetanide to furosemide equivalence first (bumetanide 1mg ≈ furosemide 40mg).

Syringe driver practical points

  • Furosemide must be used as a solitary agent — do not mix with other medications
  • Concentration: 10mg/1ml ampoules
  • T34™ syringe pump max capacity: 17ml (≈ 170mg/24h per pump)
  • Higher doses: use two pumps or 12-hourly administration (e.g. 2 × 120mg)
  • May dilute with NaCl 0.9% if syringe capacity allows, or administer undiluted
  • Protect from light; discard if solution is yellow (degradation)

Infusion sites

  • Upper chest or upper anterior arms
  • Avoid: bony prominences, damaged tissue, oedematous areas, tattoos (may mask reactions)
  • Note: very poor peripheral perfusion in terminal stage may limit SC absorption

End of life monitoring

  • Daily symptom monitoring — focus on comfort
  • Daily infusion site inspection (pain, itching, redness may require re-siting or cessation)
  • U&E monitoring as advised by VW consultant; not appropriate in the last few days of life
  • Weight monitoring also not appropriate in the last few days of life

Contraindications & precautions

For patients in the final days of life, the prescriber must consider these but they may not necessarily deter use if the dose is carefully titrated:

  • Anuria
  • Comatose/pre-comatose states with hepatic cirrhosis
  • CrCl <30ml/min per 1.73m²
  • Dehydration, hypovolaemia, or hyponatraemia
  • Drug-induced renal failure
  • Hypersensitivity to sulphonamides

📊 Ongoing Monitoring

▼

Daily monitoring

  • Heart failure symptoms: SOB, orthopnoea, PND, exercise tolerance, change in swelling, dizziness on standing
  • Blood pressure, heart rate, weight, fluid balance
  • Infusion site review (if applicable)
  • If symptomatic low BP: stop or reduce other antihypertensives
  • Target weight loss: ~500g/day

Renal function & electrolyte monitoring

U&Es every 48–72 hours (decided by specialist). Metolazone: minimum twice weekly.

Parameter Level Action Required
Potassium <3.5 mmol/L Short course of oral K+ supplements and dietary advice. If continues to reduce, consider an MRA.
>5.5–<6.0 mmol/L Stop K+ supplements, K+-sparing diuretics. Consider reducing/stopping ACEi, ARBs, MRAs, or sacubitril/valsartan. Consider haemolysis if other results stable — repeat.
>6.0 mmol/L URGENT: Repeat sampling and ECG. Discuss with senior clinical team. Refer to MERLIN hyperkalaemia guideline.
Sodium <125 mmol/L If symptomatic: urgent patient assessment, discuss with senior clinical team.
If asymptomatic: consider stopping/holding medications known to reduce sodium.
125–127 mmol/L Consider stopping medications known to reduce sodium (MRAs, ACEIs, antidepressants, PPIs). Increase U&E monitoring frequency.
Creatinine Increase >50% of baseline or >300μmol/L Reduce/stop nephrotoxic medications. If creatinine continues to increase: urgent patient assessment and discussion with senior clinical team.

⚠️ Escalation Pathway

▼
🚨 Escalate immediately if:

K+ >6.0 mmol/L • Symptomatic Na+ <125 mmol/L • Creatinine rising >50% above baseline • Uncontrolled respiratory distress • Symptomatic hypotension unresponsive to antihypertensive withdrawal

Stepwise escalation for IV diuretics

1
Increase IV furosemide by 40mg increments (maximum 240mg).
2
Add metolazone to reduce ongoing IV requirement.
3
No response within 72 hours: discuss with patient regarding wishes and goals of care.
4
Consider: comfort-focused care at home vs. further hospital-based management, guided by patient's ReSPECT form and advance care plan.

🏠 Discharge from Virtual Ward

▼

Discharge checklist

0 of 7 completed

  • IV/SC diuretics converted to appropriate oral dose
  • Other HF medications reviewed with GP advice on up-titration (ACEi/ARB, SGLT2i, MRA)
  • Current weight and target weight documented
  • Comprehensive discharge letter sent to GP
  • U&E follow-up plan documented (usually within 1–2 weeks)
  • Follow-up with local heart failure team arranged
  • Patient provided with heart failure self-management plan including diuretic plan if weight increases
